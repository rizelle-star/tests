It Took Us Seven Years to Get a Watch Case Right. I Know How That Sounds.

The plan was a sensible engineering job and a salary. Honestly, I'm still not entirely sure how it turned into this.

By Neil Ferrier, Founder of DISCOMMON
July 15, 2026
As seen in

There's a small leather case sitting on my desk right now. We call it The Puck, and one look at it tells you why. It holds exactly one watch, and it took us seven years to get right.

Most people would be embarrassed by that timeline. We're not.

But I accept it needs some explaining. Nobody sets out to spend seven years on a watch case. You have to sort of... arrive there.

So let me back up.

The plan was a salary

At the end of school I was torn between art school and mechanical engineering. My heart was with art. But I was good at physics and maths, and the adults made the traditional argument: only the immensely gifted actually make it out of art school, and engineering was the safer road into the industries I loved anyway. Automotive. Aerospace.

So I chose engineering. I wasn't thinking about being an entrepreneur back then. I was thinking about a salary.

That was the whole plan. Hold onto it, because it doesn't survive.

We owned exactly one drill

I grew up in Auchterarder, a town of about 4,000 people just outside Gleneagles in Scotland. Dad was in the golf industry, Mum was a teacher, and my childhood leaned into the Scottish cliché pretty heavily. Golf with my dad after dinner, hiking, out until nine or ten at night because the sun sits late up there in summer. The word that comes back first is freedom.

What we didn't have was tools.

The entire family workshop was one old Black & Decker drill from the 1970s. That was it. That was the collection. Dad was a fantastic businessman, but he didn't tinker. The only thing he ever worked on was his golf swing.

So I loved making things and had nothing to make them with. My outlet was paper and canvas, and everything stayed flat. Looking back, there was this wave slowly building in me. I wanted to see things come to life off the page. When I got my first car, I started inventing reasons to tinker on it.

Then I spent my beer money on a race seat

At university I found a really cheap red race seat and, in some naive fit, bought it instead of spending that week's money on drinking.

Which was somewhat useless. It just sat on the floor of my apartment.

My buddy Pete was a much more capable engineer than me and actually knew CAD, so together we sketched up a base out of mandrel-bent exhaust tubing and turned the seat into a lounge chair. We called ourselves N1 Enterprises. We sold one.

It was never going to be a real business. But you have to remember, this was long before every 12-year-old had a t-shirt company. Back then you went to college, you did college things, and then you joined the workforce. Just deciding to make something and put it into the world felt a little bit illicit.

That feeling never wore off.

Just deciding to make something and put it into the world felt a little bit illicit.

The man in the board shorts

Toward the end of university I heard a story from an associate of my dad's. He'd just come out of a meeting at Oakley's headquarters in California, and the CEO walked out of the building in board shorts and a t-shirt.

That was it — that image. That was all the market research I needed.

I decided my first job wasn't going to be somewhere boring where I'd be a junior something-or-other, a number filling a function. I applied to Oakley seven or eight times. Eventually somebody in there saw a little spark that was a bit different than the norm, and I was very, very fortunate for it.

I joined Advanced Product Development and stayed 11 years. Picture the exact opposite of a house with one drill. Astonishing designers, engineers, machines, and people with enough experience to make you believe anything could be built. Our job was to make the mad ideas real.

You do that for 11 years, and ordinary objects get very hard to ignore. In 2014, that turned into DISCOMMON.

Watch people will understand this next part

Fine watchmaking is one of the only worlds where nobody asks you to justify unreasonable effort. A component the owner may never lay eyes on gets polished, rejected, remade and polished again, purely because the people making it decided it matters. I find that genuinely reassuring about humanity.

But here's the thing that always got me. A person will own a watch worth more than their car, and then travel with it wrapped in a sock.

So we made a case for it. And because we're us, it took seven years.

The Puck

The outside is wet-formed Napa leather, skived incredibly thin so it can be formed into the kind of complex shapes we normally steal from automotive design. Inside it's compression-molded EVA foam, the shear-thickening kind that absorbs energy, lined with soft microfiber. The corners are rigid so the case can't collapse if you drop it, and the YKK zipper closes like a vault.

That's the whole thing. It holds one watch, and it might be the most over-engineered single-watch travel case ever made.

We say the fit is "universal," and the quotation marks are doing honest work. We've had Greubels, RMs and MBs in there. If your watch is stranger than that, email us first.

Meet The Puck

Now, why seven years? Because small is brutal. On a big project there's always room to hide an average decision. On something the size of a puck there's nowhere for one to go. So you adjust, and reject, and remake, well past the point where a sensible company would've shipped it.

That stubbornness didn't come from nowhere.

One missing letter

Some of my earliest DISCOMMON work was consulting for The Macallan, who paid me in whisky for almost two years. That tells you everything about how good I was at the commercial side in the early days.

We also produced a run of flasks with them, and on the finished pieces an engraved word was missing one letter.

One letter, gone. And nobody caught it until the flasks were real, sitting there, engraved.

We fixed every unit in ten days and still made the launch. I estimate that missing letter cost me around $60,000.

I'm not proud of the typo. I'm oddly proud of the ten days. And it burned one rule into the company for good: nearly right is still wrong, even when it photographs beautifully.

After that, seven years on a watch case felt entirely reasonable.
